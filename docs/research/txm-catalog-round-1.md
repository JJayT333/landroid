## TL;DR
- Texas mineral math splits into (a) a small deterministic core — intestacy fractions, the Relinquishment Act 50/50 split, acreage proration, and DOI formulas — that is **COMPUTABLE**, and (b) a large body of deed-construction doctrine (double fractions, Duhig, fixed-vs-floating royalty, "subject-to" allocation) that is frequently **STOP-AND-ASK** because the Texas Supreme Court has abandoned mechanical construction rules for four-corners intent (Wenske 2017, Hysaw 2016, Van Dyke 2023).
- The single most destabilizing modern rule is the **Van Dyke double-fraction presumption**: "1/8" inside a double fraction in an antique instrument is presumed to mean the entire mineral/royalty estate, not arithmetic 1/8. In Van Dyke itself this controlled ownership in a dispute the Texas Supreme Court described as spanning roughly ten years and about **$44 million in royalties**, turning "one-half of one-eighth" into one-half of the mineral estate (an 8x swing from the 1/16 arithmetic reading). The presumption was first successfully rebutted only on **March 13, 2026 in Clifton v. Johnson**.
- Every fraction-changing rule is cataloged with verified primary authority; where authority is split or fact-specific the entry shows **both** computations and is flagged for attorney review — the engine must never silently pick.

## Key Findings
1. **Law vs. practice diverge most at the division-order desk.** Division orders do not convey or alter title (Gavenda v. Strata, 705 S.W.2d 690 (Tex. 1986)); they bind until revoked (Exxon v. Middleton, 613 S.W.2d 240 (Tex. 1981)) EXCEPT where the payor prepared the erroneous order and retained the underpayment (unjust enrichment). Analysts book what the title opinion says, not what a signed DO says.
2. **The 9/1/1993 community-property descent change is the highest-frequency probate fork in East Texas.** Death before vs. on/after that date changes whether a surviving spouse takes the decedent's community half.
3. **Texas has no general compulsory pooling.** MIPA (Nat. Res. Code ch. 102) is narrow, never applies to state land, and never reaches reservoirs produced before 3/8/1961. Allocation/PSA wells fill the gap with acreage/lateral-length allocation that is contractual or RRC-permitted, not statutory, and lacks a controlling Texas Supreme Court holding.
4. **Relinquishment Act lands (1919) are a 50/50 split** of bonus/royalty/rental between the surface owner (as the State's agent) and the State, with a State free royalty of at least 1/16 — directly relevant in the Sam Houston National Forest counties where PSF/mineral-classified grants appear.

## SECTION 3 — TIER 1 ENTRIES (full blocks)

### TXM-001 — Duhig Over-Conveyance / Breach-of-Warranty Allocation
- **Landman statement:** A grantor who owns less than the full mineral estate signs a general warranty deed that both grants an interest AND reserves an interest, and the two together exceed what the grantor owns. Duhig fills the GRANT first out of the grantor's remaining interest; the reservation fails to the extent of the shortage. Triggered by warranty deeds, not quitclaims.
- **Deed/lease language that triggers it:** "But it is expressly agreed and stipulated that the grantor herein retains an undivided one-half interest in and to all mineral rights or minerals of whatever description in the land" — in a warranty deed where an outstanding 1/2 is already severed (the actual Duhig clause).
- **The math rule:** Let M = mineral fraction grantor actually owns; G = fraction the deed purports to grant and warrant; R = fraction reserved. If G + R > M, grantee receives G (capped at M); grantor keeps M − G; reservation honored only if surplus remains.
- **Worked example (classic Duhig):** Outstanding prior reservation = 1/2; grantor owns M = 1/2; deed grants surface + minerals warranting full title, reserves R = 1/2.
  - Grantee is warranted 1/2 of minerals → grantee gets 1/2 = 0.500000000.
  - Prior owner keeps 1/2 = 0.500000000.
  - Grantor's attempted 1/2 reservation FAILS (nothing left) → grantor = 0.000000000.
  - Check: 0.500000000 + 0.500000000 + 0.000000000 = 1.000000000. ✓
- **Worked example (Perryman exception — "except" not "reserve"):** Same facts but deed says "LESS, SAVE AND EXCEPT an undivided 1/2 of royalty." Under Perryman, prior owner (Amy) = 1/2; grantor (Ben) = 1/4; grantee (Carl) = 1/4. Carl = 0.250000000, Ben = 0.250000000, Amy = 0.500000000. Check = 1.000000000. ✓
- **Authority:** Duhig v. Peavy-Moore Lumber Co., 135 Tex. 503, 144 S.W.2d 878 (Tex. 1940); limited by Perryman v. Spartan Tex. Six Capital Partners, Ltd., 546 S.W.3d 110 (Tex. 2018).
- **Temporal applicability:** Construction principle applied today to deeds of any vintage; turns on reserve-vs-except wording. No effective-date cutoff.
- **Status:** SETTLED (with FACT-SPECIFIC reserve/except distinction).
- **Interactions:** TXM-039 (after-acquired title is the doctrinal engine), TXM-005, TXM-050.
- **Division-order practice note:** Analysts apply Duhig only on a verified warranty chain; many book the grantor's intended reservation pending a title opinion because operators fear warranty claims. Strict law fills the grant first.
- **Software mapping:** Inputs: outstanding severed fraction, grantor's owned fraction, granted fraction, reserved fraction, warranty type, reserve-vs-except flag. **STOP-AND-ASK** (reserve/except classification is interpretive).

### TXM-002 — Estate-Misconception / Double-Fraction Presumption (Van Dyke)
- **Landman statement:** An antique deed/will uses a double fraction containing 1/8 (e.g., "one-half of one-eighth"). Modern Texas law presumes the "1/8" was a term of art meaning the WHOLE mineral/royalty estate, not arithmetic 1/8. So "1/2 of 1/8" presumptively = 1/2 of the estate, NOT 1/16. The presumption is genuinely rebuttable by textual indicia.
- **Deed/lease language that triggers it:** Van Dyke deed (1924): "It is understood and agreed that one-half (1/2) of one-eighth (1/8) of all minerals and mineral rights in said land are reserved in grantors … and are not conveyed herein." Clifton deed (1951, rebutting): "a 1/128 (1/16 of the usual 1/8 royalty) part of all of the oil, gas and other minerals taken and saved under" future leases.
- **The math rule:** If double fraction = a × 1/8 in an antique instrument and the presumption is NOT rebutted: interest = a (the 1/8 collapses to "the whole"). If rebutted (e.g., a restated single product like "1/128 (1/16 of the usual 1/8)"): arithmetic applies, interest = a × 1/8.
- **Worked example (Van Dyke, presumption applies):** "1/2 of 1/8" reserved. Presumed = 1/2 of minerals = 0.500000000. (Arithmetic reading would have been 1/16 = 0.062500000 — an 8x divergence.) This single construction controlled a roughly ten-year dispute over about $44 million in royalties.
- **Worked example (Clifton v. Johnson 2026, presumption rebutted):** "1/128 (1/16 of the usual 1/8 royalty)" on Reeves County tracts. Because the parties expressly multiplied the fractions to a single restated product, the presumption was rebutted → fixed 1/128 royalty = 0.007812500 (NOT a floating 1/16 = 0.062500000).
- **Authority:** Van Dyke v. Navigator Group, No. 21-0146, 668 S.W.3d 353 (Tex. Feb. 17, 2023); Hysaw v. Dawkins, 483 S.W.3d 1 (Tex. 2016); Clifton v. Johnson, No. 23-0671 (Tex. Mar. 13, 2026) (first rebuttal); estate-misconception roots in Concord Oil v. Pennzoil, 966 S.W.2d 451 (Tex. 1998); Thomson v. Hoffman, 674 S.W.3d 927 (Tex. 2023) (remand applying Van Dyke).
- **Temporal applicability:** Per Thomson v. Hoffman progeny the presumption is not limited to a particular era ("nothing in the text of Van Dyke limits the application of the presumption to instruments of a certain era"), though the "of a certain vintage" reasoning is strongest for pre-1970 instruments.
- **Status:** SETTLED that the presumption exists and is rebuttable; FACT-SPECIFIC whether rebutted in a given instrument.
- **Interactions:** TXM-003, TXM-004.
- **Division-order practice note:** Analysts flag any "of 1/8" antique fraction for legal review and never auto-multiply.
- **Software mapping:** Inputs: instrument date, verbatim fraction string, presence of restated single product, other fractions in the instrument. **STOP-AND-ASK.**

### TXM-003 — Fixed vs. Floating Royalty
- **Landman statement:** A reserved/granted NPRI may be a FIXED fraction of gross production (e.g., always 1/16) or a FLOATING fraction OF the lease royalty (e.g., 1/2 of whatever royalty a lease reserves). With modern royalties above 1/8 the two diverge sharply.
- **Deed/lease language:** Fixed: "an undivided 1/16 royalty of all oil and gas produced." Floating: "one-half of the royalty payable under any lease."
- **The math rule:** Fixed = stated fraction of production. Floating = stated fraction × lease royalty.
- **Worked example:** Lease royalty = 1/5.
  - (a) Fixed 1/16 → 0.062500000.
  - (b) Floating "1/2 of royalty" → 1/2 × 1/5 = 1/10 = 0.100000000.
  - (c) Floating "1/2 of 1/8" under Van Dyke presumption → 1/2 of royalty = 0.100000000; arithmetic (if rebutted) → 1/16 = 0.062500000.
- **Worked example (Hahn facts):** Hahn reserved a fixed 1/8 NPRI; the Gips lease later carried a 1/4 royalty. Ratification alone left it fixed at 1/8 of production = 0.125000000. The signed Stipulation of Interest converted it to floating 1/8 of the 1/4 royalty = 1/8 × 1/4 = 0.031250000.
- **Authority:** Garrett v. Dils Co., 157 Tex. 92, 299 S.W.2d 904 (Tex. 1957) (judicial notice usual royalty = 1/8, at 907); Luckel v. White, 819 S.W.2d 459 (Tex. 1991); Hysaw v. Dawkins, 483 S.W.3d 1 (Tex. 2016); ConocoPhillips Co. v. Hahn, No. 23-0024 (Tex. Dec. 31, 2024) (ratification does not convert fixed→floating; stipulation can).
- **Temporal applicability:** Construction applied today to all vintages.
- **Status:** FACT-SPECIFIC.
- **Interactions:** TXM-002, TXM-004, TXM-027.
- **Division-order practice note:** Booking fixed vs. floating wrong is the most common multi-year underpayment; analysts compute both and escalate.
- **Software mapping:** Inputs: verbatim royalty clause, current lease royalty, ratification/stipulation flags. **STOP-AND-ASK.**

### TXM-004 — Concord Oil Two-Grant / Single-Grant Harmonization
- **Landman statement:** A single instrument states two different fractions in different clauses (e.g., a granting clause of 1/96 and a "subject-to/rentals-and-royalty" clause of 1/12). The court harmonizes them under the four-corners rule rather than mechanically preferring one clause; the differing fractions are usually multiples of 1/8.
- **Deed/lease language:** Granting clause "1/96 interest in minerals"; later clause "1/12 of all rentals and royalty of every kind and character."
- **The math rule:** No mechanical priority. Harmonize to a single intended estate; where harmonization treats the attributes of the larger fraction as controlling, the larger fraction governs (plurality result in Concord = 1/12).
- **Worked example:** Granting clause 1/96 = 0.010416667; royalty clause 1/12 = 0.083333333. Concord plurality construed the conveyance as a 1/12 interest = 0.083333333 (granting-clause 1/96 not given controlling effect). The dissent would have honored 1/96 + a separate 1/12 royalty from existing leases as two grants.
- **Authority:** Concord Oil Co. v. Pennzoil Exploration & Production Co., 966 S.W.2d 451 (Tex. 1998) (plurality); Luckel v. White, 819 S.W.2d 459 (Tex. 1991).
- **Temporal applicability:** Deeds of any vintage.
- **Status:** SPLIT AUTHORITY (plurality opinion; attorney call).
- **Interactions:** TXM-002, TXM-003.
- **Division-order practice note:** Two-fraction single instruments are escalated; both the "two-grant" and "single-grant" decimals are computed.
- **Software mapping:** Inputs: each clause's fraction, clause type, existing-lease status. **STOP-AND-ASK.**

### TXM-005 — Subject-To NPRI Burden Allocation (Wenske)
- **Landman statement:** When a deed conveys part of the minerals and reserves part, and an outstanding NPRI exists, who bears the NPRI? Default after Wenske: both grantor and grantee bear it PROPORTIONATELY to their mineral shares, unless the deed clearly puts it solely on one. The Court (5-4) held "subject to" language does not by itself burden only the grantee.
- **Deed/lease language:** "subject to the Reservations from Conveyance and the Exceptions to Conveyance and Warranty."
- **The math rule:** Each party bears NPRI pro rata: party's NPRI burden = (mineral share) × NPRI; party's net royalty share = (mineral share × lease royalty) − pro-rata NPRI.
- **Worked example (Wenske facts):** Outstanding NPRI = 1/4; Wenske reserve 3/8; Ealy 5/8.
  - Wenske bears 3/8 × 1/4 = 3/32 = 0.093750000.
  - Ealy bears 5/8 × 1/4 = 5/32 = 0.156250000.
  - NPRI total = 8/32 = 0.250000000; Wenske net mineral share = 3/8 − 3/32 = 9/32 = 0.281250000; Ealy = 5/8 − 5/32 = 15/32 = 0.468750000. Check: 9/32 + 15/32 + 8/32 = 32/32 = 1.000000000. ✓
- **Authority:** Wenske v. Ealy, 521 S.W.3d 791 (Tex. 2017); cf. Bass v. Harper, 441 S.W.2d 825 (Tex. 1969).
- **Temporal applicability:** Applied today to all deeds; the Court expressly disclaimed creating a new default rule, so it is an intent presumption.
- **Status:** FACT-SPECIFIC (5-4; drafters can override with clear language isolating the burden).
- **Interactions:** TXM-001, TXM-050.
- **Division-order practice note:** Analysts default to proportionate sharing unless the deed expressly isolates the burden on one interest.
- **Software mapping:** Inputs: NPRI fraction, each party's mineral share, deed allocation language. **STOP-AND-ASK.**

### TXM-006 — Non-Apportionment of Royalty (Japhet v. McRae)
- **Landman statement:** When leased land is subdivided after the lease, royalty is NOT shared pro rata across the new parcels; the owner of the specific tract where the producing well sits takes all the royalty (absent an entireties clause or agreement).
- **Deed/lease language:** Triggered by the absence of any apportionment/entireties clause; ordinary warranty deed subdividing leased land.
- **The math rule:** Royalty to well-tract owner = lease royalty × that owner's interest in the well tract; non-well tract owners get 0 from that well.
- **Worked example:** 15-acre lease (Japhet facts simplified to a 10-ac sale), 1/8 royalty; owner sells part, well drilled only on retained tract. Well-tract owner royalty = 1/8 = 0.125000000 of production; non-well tract = 0.000000000. With an entireties clause: each shares 1/8 by acreage, e.g., 5/10 × 1/8 = 0.062500000 each.
- **Authority:** Japhet v. McRae, 276 S.W. 669 (Tex. Comm'n App. 1925, judgm't adopted).
- **Temporal applicability:** Settled since 1925; applies to all post-lease subdivisions.
- **Status:** SETTLED.
- **Interactions:** TXM-007 (entireties clause overrides), TXM-029.
- **Division-order practice note:** Analysts confirm well-bore location vs. tract lines before paying; entireties clauses flip the math.
- **Software mapping:** Inputs: lease date vs. subdivision date, well location, entireties-clause flag. **COMPUTABLE** once well location known.

### TXM-008 — Relinquishment Act 50/50 Split + State 1/16 Free Royalty
- **Landman statement:** For PSF "mineral-classified" land sold 9/1/1895–8/21/1931, the State owns the minerals but the surface owner ("owner of the soil") is the State's statutory leasing agent and splits bonus, rental and royalty 50/50 with the State; the State's free royalty is at least 1/16. Relevant where East Texas tracts trace to mineral-classified PSF grants.
- **Deed/lease language:** GLO Relinquishment Act Lease form; patent reciting mineral classification/reservation.
- **The math rule:** Owner share = 1/2 of (bonus + rental + royalty). State free royalty ≥ 1/16 of gross; owner's royalty share = 1/2 of lease royalty.
- **Worked example:** Lease royalty = 1/5 (0.200000000). State takes 1/2 = 0.100000000; owner takes 1/2 = 0.100000000. Statutory floor: State royalty not less than 1/16 = 0.062500000 of gross on any unleased undivided interest (Para. 20, RAL form).
- **Authority:** Relinquishment Act of 1919; Greene v. Robison (Tex. 1928) (agency construction); Standard v. Sadler, 383 S.W.2d 391 (Tex. 1964); 31 Tex. Admin. Code § 9.22; Tex. Nat. Res. Code § 52.190.
- **Temporal applicability:** Land sold 9/1/1895–8/21/1931 with mineral classification. Post-8/21/1931 sales → TXM-009. Title out of the State before 9/1/1895 generally leaves no State mineral interest.
- **Status:** SETTLED.
- **Interactions:** TXM-009, TXM-010, TXM-031 (owner owes State fiduciary/executive duty).
- **Division-order practice note:** Analysts route 50% to GLO; GLO must approve the lease form or the lease is ineffective.
- **Software mapping:** Inputs: patent date, classification, lease royalty. **COMPUTABLE.**

### TXM-009 — PSF / GLO Fee Lease (post-1931 sales)
- **Landman statement:** For land patented after 8/21/1931 under the Sales and Leasing Act, the patentee owns 15/16 of the minerals and the State retains a 1/16 nonparticipating royalty; the RA agency/50-50 split does NOT apply. PSF/state-agency fee tracts are leased through the School Land Board/GLO by sealed bid.
- **Deed/lease language:** Patent reserving "a free royalty of 1/16"; GLO/PSF lease form.
- **The math rule:** Patentee mineral fraction = 15/16; State royalty = 1/16 of gross (nonparticipating, cost-free).
- **Worked example:** State royalty = 1/16 = 0.062500000 of gross production; patentee owns 15/16 = 0.937500000 of minerals (subject to its own lease royalty to a lessee).
- **Authority:** Sales and Leasing Act of 1931 (S.B. 310 / H.B. 358); Standard v. Sadler, 383 S.W.2d 391 (Tex. 1964); 31 Tex. Admin. Code § 9.22.
- **Temporal applicability:** Sales after 8/21/1931.
- **Status:** SETTLED.
- **Interactions:** TXM-008, TXM-010.
- **Division-order practice note:** State 1/16 free royalty is booked off the top, cost-free.
- **Software mapping:** Inputs: patent date, royalty reservation. **COMPUTABLE.**

### TXM-010 — Riverbed / Channel Ownership & GLO Lease
- **Landman statement:** Texas owns the beds of navigable/perennial watercourses (and certain riverbeds under the Small Bill); minerals there are leased through the GLO. Adjacent-owner boundaries and the gradient boundary make these fact-specific.
- **Deed/lease language:** GLO riverbed lease; "to the gradient boundary of the [river]."
- **The math rule:** Riverbed acreage contributed to a unit is allocated on the same acreage basis as fee tracts; ownership split between State bed and riparian uplands requires a boundary determination.
- **Worked example (MIPA riverbed):** Riverbed tract pooled into an existing unit shares on acreage; in the Ammonite/EOG proceeding the ALJs found acreage-basis allocation appropriate with a risk penalty set in the 0–100% statutory range.
- **Authority:** Tex. Nat. Res. Code ch. 51; MIPA (ch. 102) as applied in the Ammonite Oil & Gas v. EOG/RRC line; navigable-stream and Small Bill doctrine [statutory specifics **[UNVERIFIED]** in this pass — confirm the Small Bill citation before coding].
- **Temporal applicability:** Any date; boundary law applied today.
- **Status:** SETTLED (ownership) / STOP-AND-ASK (boundary & acreage).

### TXM-011 — MIPA Forced Pooling Participation & Risk Penalty
- **Landman statement:** Texas has no general compulsory pooling. MIPA permits the RRC to force-pool separately owned tracts into a proration unit on a fair-and-reasonable basis after a "fair and reasonable" voluntary offer; a non-consenting owner may bear a risk penalty (0–100% of drilling/completion cost) recovered out of production before sharing.
- **Deed/lease language:** N/A (statutory order); requires a prior documented voluntary pooling offer. MIPA does not apply to co-tenants in the same tract (needs 2+ separately owned tracts).
- **The math rule:** Acreage participation = tract acreage / unit acreage; non-consent recovery = (cost × (1 + risk penalty)) recovered from that owner's revenue share before payout.
- **Worked example:** Owner has 1 ac in a 96.32-ac unit → participation = 1/96.32 = 0.010382060. In the Finley order the unleased owners received 1/5 royalty + 4/5 WI with NO risk penalty, beginning to receive WI revenue after the operator recovered 100% of costs.
- **Authority:** Mineral Interest Pooling Act, Tex. Nat. Res. Code ch. 102 (Acts 1965; recodified Acts 1977, eff. 9/1/1977); § 102.003 (no reservoir produced before 3/8/1961); § 102.004 (not state land); RRC Finley Resources order, Docket No. 09-0252375 (2009).
- **Temporal applicability:** Applications post-1977; never reaches pre-3/8/1961 reservoirs or state land.
- **Status:** SETTLED (application is STOP-AND-ASK).
- **Interactions:** TXM-012, TXM-013, TXM-028.
- **Division-order practice note:** Force-pooled decimals come from the RRC order, not the lease.
- **Software mapping:** Inputs: unit acreage, tract acreage, risk penalty, payout status. **STOP-AND-ASK.**

### TXM-012 — Voluntary Pooled-Unit Decimal (Acreage Proration)
- **Landman statement:** In a voluntarily pooled unit, each tract's owners share unit production by the tract's acreage contribution.
- **Deed/lease language:** Lease pooling clause authorizing units of stated size.
- **The math rule:** Royalty DOI = (net mineral acres in tract / unit acres) × lease royalty × owner's fractional interest. Equivalent: (tract participation) × (owner mineral fraction) × (lease royalty).
- **Worked example:** Owner owns 1/2 mineral interest in a 40-ac tract, leased at 3/16, pooled into a 640-ac unit.
  - Net acres = 40 × 0.5 = 20; 20/640 = 0.031250000.
  - Royalty DOI = 0.031250000 × 3/16 = 0.031250000 × 0.187500000 = 0.005859375.
- **Authority:** Pooling-clause contract law; industry convention (no single controlling statute for voluntary units).
- **Temporal applicability:** Any lease with pooling authority.
- **Status:** SETTLED.
- **Interactions:** TXM-019, TXM-020, TXM-027.
- **Division-order practice note:** Standard 8-decimal DOI; deck must balance to 1.00000000.
- **Software mapping:** Inputs: net acres, unit acres, royalty, owner fraction. **COMPUTABLE.**

### TXM-013 — Allocation / PSA Well Lateral-Length Allocation
- **Landman statement:** A horizontal well crosses multiple tracts without pooling authority. Production is allocated to each tract by what can be attributed with reasonable probability — in practice usually the productive lateral length within each tract. No Texas Supreme Court holding yet validates the method.
- **Deed/lease language:** Arises where the lease has NO pooling clause or an anti-dilution clause requiring a minimum % of leased acreage in any unit; PSA wells require 65% owner consent per RRC practice.
- **The math rule:** Tract allocation factor = (productive lateral feet in tract) / (total productive lateral feet). Owner DOI = allocation factor × (net acres in tract / tract acres) × lease royalty × owner fraction.
- **Worked example (Luecke-style):** ~25% of the productive wellbore lies on owner's tracts → tract factor 0.250000000. Owner with full 1/8 royalty on its own tract → 0.250000000 × 0.125000000 = 0.031250000. (The Lueckes were limited to royalty attributable to their tracts — about 25% — not 100% of well production.)
- **Authority:** Browning Oil Co. v. Luecke, 38 S.W.3d 625 (Tex. App.—Austin 2000, pet. denied) ("reasonable probability" allocation); cf. Humble Oil & Refining Co. v. West, 508 S.W.2d 812 (Tex. 1974) ("reasonable certainty" commingling standard). Spartan/EOG and Springer Ranch settled without a SCOTX holding.
- **Temporal applicability:** Horizontal wells, ~2010 forward (first RRC allocation permit to Devon, 2010).
- **Status:** FACT-SPECIFIC (no controlling SCOTX decision; litigation risk flagged).
- **Interactions:** TXM-011, TXM-012.
- **Division-order practice note:** Operators pay on lateral-length allocation; owners may challenge under West's higher "reasonable certainty" standard.
- **Software mapping:** Inputs: per-tract productive lateral feet, total lateral, net acres, royalty. **STOP-AND-ASK.**

### TXM-014 — Intestate Descent Fractions by Death Date
- **Landman statement:** Minerals are real property and descend under the Texas intestacy statute in force at the decedent's death. The 9/1/1993 community-property change is the key fork.

- **Authority:** Tex. Estates Code §§ 201.001–201.003 (deaths on/after 9/1/1993); former Tex. Probate Code §§ 38, 45 (deaths before 9/1/1993).

### TXM-015 — Life Estate / Open-Mine Doctrine
- **Landman statement:** When a life estate exists in minerals/royalty, allocation between life tenant and remainderman depends on whether a lease or production existed when the life estate vested. Open mine → life tenant takes the whole royalty/bonus. Closed mine → royalty/bonus impounded as corpus for remaindermen; life tenant gets only the income/interest. Delay rentals go 100% to the life tenant either way.
- **Deed/lease language:** Will or deed creating "a life estate to X, remainder to Y"; or a legal life estate (e.g., surviving spouse's 1/3 life estate in separate realty).
- **The math rule:** Open mine: life tenant royalty = full royalty decimal during life. Closed mine: royalty proceeds impounded as corpus; life tenant receives only the income earned on the fund (rate = actual income on the prudently invested suspense fund — no fixed statutory percentage).
- **Worked example:** Owner's NPRI = 0.100000000 (1/2 floating of a 1/5 lease). Open mine (lease in force at vesting) → life tenant receives 0.100000000 during life, then it shifts to remainderman. Closed mine (no lease at vesting) → 0.100000000 impounded for remainderman; life tenant receives only interest earned on that fund.
- **Authority:** Youngman v. Shular, 155 Tex. 437, 288 S.W.2d 495 (Tex. 1956); Clyde v. Hamilton, 414 S.W.2d 434 (Tex. 1967); Mitchell v. Mitchell, 157 Tex. 346, 303 S.W.2d 352 (Tex. 1957); Moore v. Vines, 474 S.W.2d 437 (Tex. 1971).
- **Temporal applicability:** Life estates created any date; "open vs. closed" status fixed at the moment the life estate vests. Texas treats a mere existing lease (not only an actually-producing well) as an "open mine."
- **Status:** SETTLED (allocation mechanics STOP-AND-ASK).
- **Interactions:** TXM-014, TXM-016, TXM-030.
- **Division-order practice note:** Operators suspend or split; analysts confirm whether a lease was in force at vesting. No single Texas case fixes a numeric interest rate — do not hardcode one.
- **Software mapping:** Inputs: vesting date, lease/production status at vesting, life-tenant identity, fund interest rate. **STOP-AND-ASK.**

### TXM-019 — Division-Order DOI Formula
- **Landman statement:** The decimal of interest (DOI) on a division order translates an owner's title fraction into a share of unit revenue. Royalty, NPRI, ORRI, and WI use distinct formulas; the deck must balance to 1.00000000.
- **Deed/lease language:** N/A — accounting from the title opinion.
- **The math rule:**
  - Royalty DOI = (net mineral acres / unit acres) × lease royalty × owner fraction.
  - NPRI DOI (fixed) = NPRI fraction × (tract acres / unit acres).
  - NPRI DOI (floating) = NPRI-of-royalty fraction × lease royalty × (tract acres / unit acres).
  - ORRI DOI = ORRI fraction × (tract acres / unit acres) (carved from WI; reduces lessee NRI, not lessor royalty).
  - WI NRI = WI fraction × (1 − total lease burdens) × tract participation.
- **Worked example (single tract, 1/5 royalty, 1 ORRI of 1/40):** Royalty = 0.200000000; ORRI = 0.025000000; WI NRI = 1 − 0.200000000 − 0.025000000 = 0.775000000. Check = 1.000000000. ✓
- **Authority:** NADOA Model Form Division Order (adopted 9/1995); industry convention; Exxon v. Middleton, 613 S.W.2d 240 (Tex. 1981) (DO effect).
- **Temporal applicability:** Any producing property.
- **Status:** SETTLED.
- **Interactions:** TXM-012, TXM-020, TXM-021, TXM-022.
- **Division-order practice note:** Carry full precision; round only the final DOI to 8 places; allocate the residual to the largest interest so the deck sums exactly to 1.00000000.
- **Software mapping:** Inputs: net acres, unit acres, royalty, burdens, owner fraction, interest type. **COMPUTABLE.**

### TXM-021 — Gavenda Underpayment / DO-Not-Binding Rule
- **Landman statement:** Division and transfer orders are binding until revoked and do not amend the lease or deed. The exception: where the payor itself prepared an erroneous DO and RETAINED the underpaid amount (unjust enrichment), the DO does not bind the underpaid owner.
- **Deed/lease language:** N/A.
- **The math rule:** If payor retained the difference, owner recovers (true decimal − paid decimal) × revenue; payor is NOT liable for amounts it correctly paid out to other royalty/override owners.
- **Worked example (Gavenda):** Deed reserved a 1/2 royalty (0.500000000); operator's DO paid 1/16 (0.062500000). Underpayment decimal retained by payor = 0.500000000 − 0.062500000 = 0.437500000 (the 7/16 the operator kept). Owner recovers on that 7/16; not on amounts paid to other override owners out of the working interest.
- **Authority:** Gavenda v. Strata Energy, Inc., 705 S.W.2d 690 (Tex. 1986); Exxon Corp. v. Middleton, 613 S.W.2d 240 (Tex. 1981).
- **Temporal applicability:** Any date.
- **Status:** SETTLED (application is STOP-AND-ASK — requires tracing who retained the funds).
- **Interactions:** TXM-019, TXM-022.
- **Division-order practice note:** Analysts treat the title-opinion decimal as controlling over a signed DO; a DO is not a conveyance.
- **Software mapping:** Inputs: true decimal, paid decimal, who retained funds. **STOP-AND-ASK.**

### TXM-022 — Late-Payment Interest & Timing (Nat. Res. Code 91.402–.404)
- **Landman statement:** Statutory deadlines govern first and subsequent royalty payments; late payments accrue statutory interest. A payor may withhold without interest for enumerated causes (title dispute, unsatisfied title-opinion requirement, etc.).
- **Deed/lease language:** Lease may set its own payment time; statute is the default gap-filler.
- **The math rule:** Default first payment within 120 days after the end of the month of first sale; thereafter 60 days (oil) / 90 days (gas) after the month of sale if the lease is silent. Late interest: 2 percentage points above the NY Fed discount rate (rising to higher steps as the statute provides).
- **Worked example:** If lease is silent and oil first sold in January, first payment due within 120 days of January month-end; a subsequent monthly oil payment for March sales is due within 60 days of March month-end. Interest accrues on overdue amounts at the statutory rate (2 points over NY Fed) until paid.
- **Authority:** Tex. Nat. Res. Code §§ 91.402, 91.403, 91.404; SB 1259 (eff. 5/24/2021) limiting common-law breach claims where the statute applies.
- **Temporal applicability:** Production after the relevant dates; SB 1259 reaches disputes after 5/24/2021.
- **Status:** SETTLED.
- **Interactions:** TXM-019, TXM-021.
- **Division-order practice note:** Analysts track suspense reasons (title dispute, unsigned DO, unsatisfied title-opinion requirement) that legitimately stop interest.
- **Software mapping:** Inputs: first-sale date, product, lease payment terms, NY Fed rate, suspense reason. **COMPUTABLE.**

### TXM-023 — ORRI Assignment Scope (Piranha)
- **Landman statement:** An assignment of an overriding royalty that references a well, a tract, and a lease may convey the override under the ENTIRE lease, not just the named wellbore or quarter-section. The court harmonizes all provisions holistically.
- **Deed/lease language:** "all of [Assignor's] right, title and interest in and to the properties described in Exhibit A … INSOFAR AND ONLY INSOFAR AS set out in Exhibit A … which shall include any … overriding royalty interests … held by Assignor as of the Effective Date."
- **The math rule:** Scope determines the acreage/lease base to which the override fraction attaches; the fraction itself (e.g., 3.75%) is unchanged, but the productive base can be the whole lease.
- **Worked example (Piranha):** Override = 3.75% = 0.037500000. Construed lease-wide, it attaches to production from ALL wells on Section 28, not just the Puryear B #1-28. Mis-booking it wellbore-only would zero out the override on later wells — a 100% error on those wells.
- **Authority:** Piranha Partners v. Neuhoff, 596 S.W.3d 740 (Tex. 2020); Luckel v. White, 819 S.W.2d 459 (Tex. 1991).
- **Temporal applicability:** Assignments any date.
- **Status:** SETTLED (holistic construction; application STOP-AND-ASK).
- **Interactions:** TXM-024, TXM-032.
- **Division-order practice note:** Where an exhibit names only a well and land but NOT a lease, the lease-wide result may not follow — escalate.
- **Software mapping:** Inputs: granting-clause text, exhibit description (well/land/lease), "insofar" language. **STOP-AND-ASK.**

### TXM-024 — ORRI / Royalty Post-Production Cost Bearing
- **Landman statement:** Whether post-production costs (gathering, compression, treating, transport) reduce a royalty/override decimal's dollar value turns on the valuation point and the exact clause language. "At the well"/"into the pipeline" → costs deductible; properly drafted cost-free language → not deductible. This changes dollars, not the fraction.
- **Deed/lease language:** Deductible: "into the pipelines, tanks or other receptacles with which the wells may be connected." Cost-free (if drafted right): "cost-free (except only its portion of production taxes)."
- **The math rule:** Net value = decimal × (sales price − allocated PPC) when valued at the well; = decimal × sales price when cost-free.
- **Worked example:** ORRI decimal 0.037500000, downstream sale $10.00/unit, PPC $1.20/unit. Deductible: 0.037500000 × (10.00 − 1.20) = $0.330000 per unit. Cost-free: 0.037500000 × 10.00 = $0.375000 per unit.
- **Authority:** Heritage Resources, Inc. v. NationsBank, 939 S.W.2d 118 (Tex. 1996); Chesapeake Exploration v. Hyder, 483 S.W.3d 870 (Tex. 2016); Burlington Resources Oil & Gas Co. v. Texas Crude Energy, 573 S.W.3d 198 (Tex. 2019).
- **Temporal applicability:** Leases/assignments any date.
- **Status:** SETTLED (clause-dependent; application STOP-AND-ASK).
- **Interactions:** TXM-023.
- **Division-order practice note:** This is a value calc, not a decimal calc — keep PPC logic separate from DOI.
- **Software mapping:** Inputs: valuation-point language, cost-free flag, sales price, PPC. **STOP-AND-ASK.**

### TXM-027 — NPRI / Unleased-Owner Pooling Ratification (Montgomery v. Rittersbacher)
- **Landman statement:** An NPRI owner is not bound by the executive's pooling unless the NPRI owner ratifies (expressly or by accepting pooled benefits). Once ratified, the NPRI shares in pooled production on a unit-acreage basis rather than only on the tract where a well sits.
- **Deed/lease language:** Ratification instrument, or constructive ratification by accepting pooled royalty.
- **The math rule:** Before ratification: NPRI paid only on production physically from its tract. After ratification: NPRI shares unit production by (tract acres / unit acres).
- **Worked example:** NPRI = 1/16 (fixed). Unratified, well off-tract → 0.000000000 to NPRI. Ratified, tract is 80 of 640 unit acres → 0.062500000 × (80/640) = 0.062500000 × 0.125000000 = 0.007812500.
- **Authority:** Montgomery v. Rittersbacher, 424 S.W.2d 210 (Tex. 1968); ConocoPhillips Co. v. Hahn, No. 23-0024 (Tex. Dec. 31, 2024) (ratification alone does not convert fixed NPRI to floating).
- **Temporal applicability:** Any date.
- **Status:** SETTLED (ratification fact STOP-AND-ASK).
- **Interactions:** TXM-003, TXM-012.
- **Division-order practice note:** Track whether each NPRI owner has ratified; per Hahn, ratification does not change a fixed NPRI to floating.
- **Software mapping:** Inputs: ratification flag, NPRI type/fraction, tract & unit acres. **STOP-AND-ASK.**

### TXM-037 — Strip-and-Gore / Centerline Presumption
- **Landman statement:** A conveyance of land abutting a road/strip is presumed to carry title (including minerals) to the centerline (or the whole narrow strip) unless the grantor expressly and specifically reserves it and the strip retained practical value to the grantor. This can silently add or remove acreage/minerals under roads.
- **Deed/lease language:** Triggered by deeds describing tracts "bounded by" a highway with no express reservation of the strip; rebutted only by plain, specific reservation.
- **The math rule:** If the doctrine applies, net mineral acres include the proportional strip/centerline area; if rebutted, they do not.
- **Worked example (Green v. Chesapeake):** A 30.591-ac highway tract (≈55% of a conveyed 54.95-ac tract) passed with the adjacent conveyance because the severed minerals were inaccessible/valueless at the 1972 conveyance — adding ~30.591 net mineral acres to the grantee's holding.
- **Authority:** Strip-and-gore doctrine (Texas common law since ~1862); Green v. Chesapeake Exploration, L.L.C., No. 02-17-00405-CV, 2018 WL 6565790 (Tex. App.—Fort Worth Dec. 13, 2018, no pet.) (mem. op.); Escondido Servs., LLC v. VKM Holdings, LP (Tex. App.—Eastland 2010); Angelo v. Biscamp, 441 S.W.2d 524 (Tex. 1969).
- **Temporal applicability:** Applied to deeds of any date; value judged at time of conveyance.
- **Status:** SETTLED (highly fact-driven application).
- **Interactions:** TXM-038, TXM-010.
- **Division-order practice note:** Analysts re-survey net acres under roads; horizontal-era value can resurrect formerly "valueless" strips.
- **Software mapping:** Inputs: deed description, abutting strip dimensions, express-reservation flag, strip value at conveyance. **STOP-AND-ASK.**

### TXM-040 — Correction Instruments (Prop. Code 5.027–.031)
- **Landman statement:** Recorded errors can be fixed by a correction instrument: nonmaterial (clerical) corrections by anyone with personal knowledge; material corrections require the original parties (or, if applicable, heirs/successors). A correction relates back to the original effective date but cannot add property never conveyed.
- **Deed/lease language:** "Correction [Special Warranty] Deed" reciting the error and correction.
- **The math rule:** A valid correction changes the fraction/description as of the original instrument's date; a defective one (e.g., adding an omitted tract) is void as a correction and requires a new conveyance.
- **Worked example:** A 1/4 vs. 1/2 scrivener's error in a reserved fraction, corrected under §5.029, books as the corrected fraction effective the original date; the engine should re-run downstream decimals from that date forward.
- **Authority:** Tex. Prop. Code §§ 5.027–5.031 (Acts 2011, S.B. 1496, eff. 9/1/2011); Broadway Nat'l Bank v. Yates Energy Corp., 631 S.W.3d 16 (Tex. 2021); Myrad Properties, Inc. v. LaSalle Bank, 300 S.W.3d 746 (Tex. 2009).
- **Temporal applicability:** §5.031 reaches corrections recorded before 9/1/2011 that substantially complied.
- **Status:** SETTLED (material/nonmaterial classification STOP-AND-ASK).
- **Interactions:** TXM-001, TXM-039.
- **Division-order practice note:** Title standards demand signatures beyond the statutory minimum; analysts treat unsigned/under-signed corrections cautiously.
- **Software mapping:** Inputs: error type, signatories, recording date, prior-instrument date. **STOP-AND-ASK.**

### TXM-041 — Adverse Possession of Severed Minerals
- **Landman statement:** Once minerals are severed, possessing the surface never adversely possesses the minerals. Severed working interests can be adversely possessed only by actual drilling and production for the full limitations period; royalty/NPRI/ORRI (non-possessory) cannot be adversely possessed at all.
- **Deed/lease language:** N/A — turns on conduct and severance.
- **The math rule:** A recorded mineral/royalty fraction does not change by limitations through surface use, lease execution, or royalty receipt. A WI fraction can shift only via hostile drilling/production for the statutory term.
- **Worked example:** A farms B's surface 30 years over B's severed 1/8 mineral interest → B's 0.125000000 unchanged. If A instead drills and produces hostilely for the statutory term, A may perfect the producing mineral interest by limitations.
- **Authority:** Natural Gas Pipeline Co. of America v. Pool, 124 S.W.3d 188, 192–93 (Tex. 2003); Pewitt v. Renwar Oil Corp., 152 Tex. 387, 261 S.W.2d 904 (Tex. 1953); limitations periods Tex. Civ. Prac. & Rem. Code §§ 16.024–16.028. PBEX II, LLC v. Dorchester Minerals, L.P. (Tex. App.—Amarillo 2023) on non-operating WI **[UNVERIFIED — confirm citation and review status before relying]**.
- **Temporal applicability:** Any date.
- **Status:** SETTLED.
- **Interactions:** TXM-028, TXM-042.
- **Software mapping:** Inputs: severance date, nature of possession, production dates, interest type. **STOP-AND-ASK.**

| "one-third (1/3) of an undivided one-eighth (1/8) royalty" (will) | 1/24 fixed = 0.041666667 | 1/3 floating royalty → 1/3 × 1/5 = 0.066666667 | Hysaw, 483 S.W.3d 1 (Tex. 2016) | 0.04167 vs 0.06667 |

## SECTION 5 — PROBATE / HEIRSHIP MATH PACK

**Intestate descent of real property (minerals are realty), by death date:**

- **Married, all children also of surviving spouse, death on/after 9/1/1993:** Decedent's community half → 100% to surviving spouse (Est. Code 201.003(b)(2)). Decedent's separate realty → children take all subject to spouse's 1/3 life estate (Est. Code 201.002(b)).
- **Married, ≥1 child NOT of surviving spouse, on/after 9/1/1993:** Decedent's community half → decedent's children equally; spouse retains own half (Est. Code 201.003(c)).
- **Death before 9/1/1993 (any children):** Decedent's community half → children equally; spouse keeps own half (former Probate Code §45).
- **Separate real property, spouse + children:** spouse 1/3 life estate, children 2/3 plus remainder (Est. Code 201.002(b)).
- **No spouse, children only:** children take all equally.
- **No spouse/descendants:** to parents/siblings per Est. Code 201.001 (parents 1/2 each; if one parent dead, that half to siblings/their descendants; if no parents, all to siblings).

**Worked tree (per capita with representation / per stirpes), Est. Code 201.101:**
Decedent owns 1.000000000 mineral interest; 3 children; child B predeceased leaving 2 grandchildren.
- Surviving child A = 1/3 = 0.333333333
- Surviving child C = 1/3 = 0.333333333
- B's 1/3 split between 2 grandchildren = 1/6 each = 0.166666667 each
- Exact-fraction check: 1/3 + 1/3 + 1/6 + 1/6 = 1 ✓. (At 9 decimals the sum is 1.000000000 only after allocating the +0.000000001 rounding residual to the largest share — apply the deck-balancing convention so the booked deck sums to exactly 1.000000000.)

**Half-blood (Est. Code 201.057):** a collateral half-blood takes half the share of whole-blood. One whole sibling (W) and one half sibling (H): ratio 2:1 → W = 2/3 = 0.666666667, H = 1/3 = 0.333333333.

**Adopted child (201.054):** inherits as a natural child. **120-hour survival (121.052):** an heir failing to survive 120 hours is treated as predeceased. **Homestead:** a probate homestead right does NOT change the underlying mineral fraction — it is a possessory surface right only.

**Affidavit-of-heirship reliance limit:** an AOH is prima facie evidence and, after 5 years of record (Est. Code 203.001), raises a presumption, but it is rebuttable and is NOT a conveyance. STOP-AND-ASK where an AOH conflicts with recorded deeds.

## SECTION 6 — DIVISION ORDER / NADOA PRACTICE PACK

**Core DOI formulas (8-decimal industry standard):** see TXM-019.

**Check totals:** Σ (all lessor royalties + ORRIs + WI NRIs) on a unit must equal 1.00000000.

**Worked deck balance (single tract, 1/5 royalty, 1/40 ORRI):**
- Royalty owners total = 0.200000000
- ORRI = 0.025000000 (carved from WI)
- WI NRI = 1 − 0.200000000 − 0.025000000 = 0.775000000
- Check = 1.000000000 ✓

**Rounding convention:** carry full precision internally; round only the final DOI to 8 places; allocate the residual to the largest interest so the deck sums to exactly 1.00000000.

**Proportionate reduction (lesser-interest clause, TXM-020):** if the lessor owns less than the full mineral estate, royalty is reduced proportionally. Owner of 1/2 mineral, lease 3/16 → royalty = 0.5 × 0.187500000 = 0.093750000.

**Payout flips (TXM-036):** before payout the carrying WI bears 100% of cost and earns the carried party's share; after payout it reverts per the JOA (e.g., 100% BPO → 75% APO). Engine needs the payout balance and the APO fraction.

**Top-lease / unleased-owner handling:** an unleased mineral owner shares as a cost-bearing co-tenant after the operator recoups reasonable costs (TXM-028); a top lease (note the Prop. Code §5.152 14-point "THIS IS NOT AN OIL AND GAS LEASE" caption requirement, eff. 9/1/2019, for royalty/mineral conveyances) does not attach until the base lease expires.

**Non-participating vs. participating conventions:** NPRI bears no costs of leasing/bonus/delay rental and has no executive right; participating royalty/mineral interests share bonus and rentals.

## SECTION 7 — TIER 2 INVENTORY (name / math effect / source / trigger dates)

- **Federal communitization (BLM):** federal lease tracts share production by acreage in a Communitization Agreement; allocation = tract acres / CA acres. Source: 43 CFR Part 3105. Trigger: federal lease in the unit. **Federal preemption applies.**
- **ONRR valuation:** federal royalty value set by 30 CFR Part 1206 (gross proceeds/index); affects royalty dollars, not the fraction. Federal preemption.
- **Federal unit participation:** the participating-area (PA) formula in the unit agreement governs; PA expansions change tract factors over time.
- **New Mexico state (NMSLO):** state royalty set by lease (commonly 1/8–3/16+); New Mexico HAS compulsory pooling under N.M. Stat. § 70-2-17 (unlike Texas). Trigger: NM state tracts.
- **New Mexico fee:** community-property state; intestacy under the NM Uniform Probate Code differs from Texas — do not reuse Texas descent fractions.
- **Indian lands (out of product scope — one paragraph):** BIA-approved leases (25 CFR Parts 211 (tribal) / 212 (allotted)), trust vs. allotted ownership, and federal trust valuation control; fractionated allotments can have hundreds of owners. This is federal/trust law and out of LANDroid's product scope — do not implement Tier-2 Indian-land math without dedicated federal research.

4. **Treat Van Dyke/Clifton as a live, evolving area.** Re-verify after each Texas Supreme Court term; the rebuttal standard is only one case old (March 13, 2026, Clifton v. Johnson). Keep the double-fraction module's presumption toggle and rebuttal-indicia checklist data-driven.

## Caveats
- This catalog prioritizes the math-changing doctrines on the seed list and checklist; several Tier-1 IDs are summarized rather than fully blocked for length, but all carry verified primary authority or are explicitly labeled "industry convention (no controlling law)."
- Texas deed construction is deliberately anti-mechanical post-2016 (Hysaw, Wenske, Piranha, Van Dyke). Any entry marked FACT-SPECIFIC means the same words can yield different decimals in different instruments — the engine must not hardcode an outcome.
- Citations marked **[UNVERIFIED]** were not independently confirmed in a primary reporter during this pass and must be checked before use. All other case citations were verified against court opinions (txcourts.gov, FindLaw, Justia, CourtListener) or cross-checked across multiple reputable legal sources.
- Where a worked example shows a 9-decimal residual (e.g., 1/3 and 1/6 trees), the residual is a rounding artifact of fixed 9-decimal display, not a math error; the exact-fraction sum is 1. The engine should keep rational arithmetic internally and only round at output, allocating residuals by the deck-balancing convention.
- Federal / ONRR / BIA and New Mexico matters are inventory-only and frequently preempt or differ from Texas law; do not implement Tier-2 math without dedicated jurisdiction-specific research.
